### Gerunds and Gerundives

Gerund is a verbal noun (as in 'I like *walking*.') and gerundive is a verbal adjective.

> **amāre dulce est.**  
> Loving is delightful.

> **ars amandī**  
> the art of loving

> **studuit amandō.**  
> He devoted himself to loving.

> **nescit amāre.**  
> He does not know how to love. Literally, He does not know loving.

In Latin the present infinitive active supplies the nominative, and the accusative (but not when after prepositions governing the accusative, in which case *-andum* or *-endum* is used) case of the gerund. 

> **parātus ad amandum**  
> ready for loving

In other cases *-andī* or *-andō* is added to the stem of 1st conjugation verbs, and *-endī* or *-endō* the other conjugations, including deponents.

| case | 1st conjugation                | 2nd conjugation              | 3rd conjugation          | 4th conjugation           |
|------|--------------------------------|------------------------------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| nom. | parāre (to prepare, preparing) | monēre (to advise, advising) | regere (to rule, ruling) | audīre (to hear, hearing) |
| gen. | parandī                        | monendī                      | regendī                  | audiendī                  |
| dat. | parandō                        | monendō                      | regendō                  | audiendō                  |
| acc. | parāre, parandum               | monēre, monendum             | regere, regendum         | audīre, audiendum         |
| abl. | parandō                        | monendō                      | regendō                  | audiendō                  |

The gerund is neuter.

There is no gerundive usage in English or almost no other language than Latin, but it can be interpreted as a future passive participle, which translates as follows:

> **liber legendus**  
> a book to be read, a book needing to be read, a must-read book

#### Gerundival attraction

> **fēminās amandō**  
> by loving women

> **ars cīuēs regendī**  
> the art of ruling the citizens

These two phrases are not ideal Latin, for, unlike in English, where the gerund can take an object, Latin usually prefers to harmonize the endings of the gerund and of its object.

After that we have the transformed and 'harmonized' gerund as a adjective, ending in *-andus, -a, -um* or *-endus, -a, -um*, and it is called gerundive.

> **fēminīs amandīs**  
> by loving women

> **ars cīuium regendōrum**  
> the art of ruling the citizens

Note: 

1. Gerundive atraction always occurs:

   - after prepositions;
   - when the gerund is in the dative.

2. Gerundival attraction tends to be avoided 

   - with neuter pronouns or adjectives in the genitive, dative, or ablative;
   - when the meaning of the verb is stressed;
   - by some authors when it would lead to a repetition of first and second declension endings in the genitive plural.

   Thus *ars librōs legendī* may be preferable to the cumbrous *ars librōrum legendōrum*.

Note:

1. *ad* + gerund/gerundive expresses purpose:

   > **ad pācem petendam**  
   > for the purpose of seeking peace

2. The postpositions *causā* and *grātiā* (both with the genitive) expresses 'for the sake of':

   > **pācis petendae causā (*or* grātiā)**  
   > for the sake of seeking peace, i.e. in order to seek peace

3. Nouns which take a 'determining' genitive, such as *occāsiō, occāsiōnis, f.* (opportunity), *facultās, facultātis, f.* (opportunity), *signum, signī, n.* (sign, signal), and adjectives which govern a noun in the genitive, such as *cupidus, -a, -um* (eager) and *perītus, -a… -um* (skilled), are followed by the genitive of the gerund or gerundive.

   > **occāsiō amandī**  
   > an opportunity for loving, i.e. a chance to love

   > **signum oppugnandī**  
   > the signal to attack

   > **signum oppidī oppugnandī**  
   > the signal to attack the town

   > **cupidus edendī**  
   > eager to eat (literally, desirous of eating)

   > **perītus docendī**  
   > experienced in teaching

4. Note the following usages with verbs of entrusting and undertaking, e.g. *dō, dare, dedī, datum* (I give), *cūrō* (1) (I see to) and *suscipiō, suscipere, susceptum* (I undertake):

   > **librum legendum puellae dedī**  
   > I gave the girl a book to read.

   > **pontem aedificandum cūrāuit.**  
   > He saw to the building of the bridge.

   > **suscēpit nāuēs reficiendās.**  
   > He undertook the repair of the ships.

5. The gerund of *eō* (I go) is *eundum*. The form *faciundum*, instead of *faciendum*, from *faciō* (I make, do) is common in old Latin. Note also the expression *(pecūniae) repetundae*, f.pl., meaning 'extortion of money'.

#### The gerundive of obligation

When the gerundive is combined with the verb *esse* (to be) it expresses ideas of obligation (e.g. 'ought', 'must', 'should', 'have to'), and is therefore called 'the gerundive of obligation'.

##### The gerundive with transitive verbs

> **librum legō.**  
> I read the book

> **liber legitur.**  
> The book is read.

> **liber legendus est.**  
> The book is to-be-read, i.e. The book must be (should be, ought to be, has to be) read.

> **liber legendus erat.**  
> The book was to-be-read, i.e. The book had to be read.

When negatived the gerundive can express ideas of permissibility or appropriateness:

> **rēs nōn contemnenda**  
> a thing not to be despised

Remember that the gerundive is passive in meaning.

The agent should be in the dative:

> **liber mihi legendus est.**  
> The book is to-be-read by me, i.e. I must read the book.

> **puella tibi amanda est.**  
> You must love the girl.

> **urbs nōbīs relinquenda est.**  
> We must leave the city.

Note that this pattern is followed only with transitive verbs whose objects are in the accusative, for others see below.

##### The gerundive with intransitive verbs

The gerundive of intransitive verbs and verbs followed by cases other than the accusative must be used **impersonnaly**.

> **ab urbe nōbīs discēdendum est.**  
> It is to-be-left from the city by us, i.e. We must leave the city. (Compare the last example in the last section)

> **prīmā lūce nōbīs prōgrediendum est.**  
> We must set out at dawn.

> **mihi errantī ignōscendum est.**  
> I must be forgiven when I make a mistake.

> **lēgibus pārendum est.**  
> The laws must be obeyed.

The agent normally is in the dative but when the object of the verb is also in the dative, an ambiguity would arise:

> **lēgibus nōbīs pārendum est.**  
> 'We must obey the laws.' **or** 'The laws must obey us.' **?**

In such cases *ā* or *ab* is used with the ablative of agent:

> **lēgibus ā nōbīs pārendum est.**
